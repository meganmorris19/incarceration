Female Incarceration and the War on Drugs

By MEGAN MORRIS     MARCH 28, 2016


According to The Sentencing Project's 2012 "Incarcerated Women Factsheet," the number of females incarcerated in the United States increased by 587% from 1980 to 2011. Policies associated with the War on Drugs (a campaign started by President Richard Nixon in 1971) significantly increased the incarceration rate in the U.S. These policies disproportionately target women — many of whom have children and struggle with mental illness and substance abuse.

In 2015, the United States imprisoned more women than any other country in the world, with 205,400 total females incarcerated (approximately a third of the world's total). In 2014, the majority of incarcerated females were convicted of drug violations. In 1986, near the beginning of the War on Drugs, the majority of incarcerated females were charged with violent crimes or property crimes.

Female inmates have higher rates of mental illness, HIV/AIDS and drug dependence than their male counterparts. Approximately 60% of female offenders suffered physical or sexual abuse prior to incarceration, greater than 50% suffer from mental illness, and approximately 62% are mothers of minor children. Additionally, conspiracy laws designed to implicate all individuals regardless of their level of involvement criminalize women living under the same roof as a drug-dealing boyfriend or husband. Between 1980 and 2010, the number of female inmates increased by 646%, 1.5 times the rate of men.

"The number of women and girls in prison has increased by 50% since about the year 2000. This cannot be explained in terms of the growth in national population levels: United Nations figures for the world population indicate that this rose only by 18% in about the same time period."

— Institute for Criminal Policy Research
